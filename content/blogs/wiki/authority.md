# Authority

**Summary**: Every worldview submits to some authority; the blog argues that Jesus is the right one, and that submitting to his Royal Law of love is the source of Christian identity, freedom, and ethical coherence.

## Authority Is Not Neutral

Every claim about truth and reality represents submission to some authority — reason, tradition, community, self, scripture, Jesus. The contemporary default of "you do you" is not the absence of authority; it is self-authority elevated to ultimacy (source: [2021-11-23-on-authority.md](../raw/2021-11-23-on-authority.md)).

This matters because it frames the Christian claim not as anti-intellectual or anti-freedom, but as a rival answer to a question everyone answers: *whose word settles things for you?*

## Jesus as Ultimate Authority

The blog identifies Jesus as the appropriate authority for Christians — not because he is demanding, but because his authority takes the form of the **Royal Law**: "Love your neighbor as yourself" (James 2:8; Matthew 22:37-40).

Submitting to Jesus means submitting to love as the ordering principle of life. This is not tyranny; it is alignment with reality as God designed it. "He made a way, and he is the way." (source: [2026-04-07-the-first-way.md](../raw/2026-04-07-the-first-way.md))

## Freedom Requires Responsibility

A consistent thread: freedom and responsibility are inseparable. When one party in a relationship abdicates responsibility, the other must impose external constraint (source: [2018-02-20-lead-follow-or-accept-restraint.md](../raw/2018-02-20-lead-follow-or-accept-restraint.md)):

> "When shepherds stop leading or sheep stop following, secure pens become inevitable."

The surest way to lose freedom is to neglect responsibility. This applies in relationships, communities, and societies. The Gospel and [[kingdom-of-god]] culture are presented as the practical solution to this pattern — not because of coercion but because Kingdom identity orients people toward self-giving rather than self-protection.

## Anticipating Error

In relationships and community life, anticipating error and providing "a bigger margin of grace" is wiser than expecting perfect alignment. "If one of us swerves, we're going to crash" describes rigidity; grace-based authority structures build in room for failure without abandoning accountability (source: [2017-10-07-when-christians-dont-hold-the-line.md](../raw/2017-10-07-when-christians-dont-hold-the-line.md)).

## Tribal Authority vs. Gospel Conviction

A recurring danger: acting rightly for the wrong reasons — because one's tribe demands it rather than because of Gospel conviction. When every issue divides into "People Like Us" and "People Not Like Us," tribal pressure becomes a de facto authority overriding conscience.

The alternative: "Do the right things for our own reasons" — rooting actions in the Gospel first, not in what the tribe approves. This is a form of sovereignty under Christ rather than under tribe (source: [2017-08-23-doing-the-right-things-for-our-own-reasons.md](../raw/2017-08-23-doing-the-right-things-for-our-own-reasons.md)).
