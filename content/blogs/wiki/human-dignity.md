# Human Dignity

**Summary**: Human dignity — rooted in the image of God (*imago Dei*) — is both the foundation of Christian ethics and the primary target of all sin; restoring it is the core of Kingdom work.

## Dehumanization as the Root of All Sin

Drawing on Dean Sherman's claim that "all problems are relational," the blog argues that dehumanization is the engine behind every form of sin and injustice (source: [2019-09-16-every-problem-in-the-world.md](../raw/2019-09-16-every-problem-in-the-world.md)):

> Violence, injustice, and abuse become possible only when we first diminish another's humanity.

The logical chain: I reduce you from a full person with history, family, and dignity to a type, a threat, a resource — and then harm becomes thinkable. This applies from gossip (reducing someone to their worst moment) to genocide (reducing a people to a category).

The counter-movement is **re-humanization**: actively restoring and seeing the full humanity of others through Christ's love.

## People Stewardship vs. Human Resources

The shift from "Personnel" to "Human Resources" in organizational language marked a dehumanizing move — treating people as commodities alongside cash, inventory, and machinery (source: [2017-07-05-rehumanizing.md](../raw/2017-07-05-rehumanizing.md)).

"People stewardship must look different than the stewardship of non-human resources." The alternative is stewarding *influence* — the relational trust and impact we have with people — rather than managing people as inputs.

This applies in workplaces, [[community]] structures, and church leadership. A "two-tiered humanity" where rights belong only to the powerful is a direct contradiction of the Gospel.

The logic extends to economic metrics: measuring human contribution by Labor Force Participation Rate reduces persons to their paycheck-earning status — the same reductive move in economic language. A 2026 post argues that trust-builders who earn no wage may contribute more to human flourishing than employed people who erode social trust (source: [2026-04-21-who-contributes-more?.md](../raw/2026-04-21-who-contributes-more?.md)). Dehumanization by economic metric is still dehumanization.

## Care for the Vulnerable

Stewardship of creation includes care for the weak and vulnerable — a theme running from Genesis 1 (dominion as responsibility) through the prophetic tradition to Jesus' ministry. A worm rescued from desiccating concrete by a compassionate builder becomes an image of what Christlike stewardship looks like: attending to the small and helpless (source: [2017-07-12-survival-of-the-weakest.md](../raw/2017-07-12-survival-of-the-weakest.md)).

This is not sentiment. It is the practical expression of seeing every creature as bearing God's intention and every person as bearing God's image.

## Truth and Dignity Together

[[Justice and mercy]] are not competing priorities when human dignity is the frame. Joseph's response to discovering Mary's pregnancy (Matthew 1) models holding both: he could have exposed her publicly, but instead planned to act privately — protecting her dignity while still acknowledging truth (source: [2017-06-10-just-mercy.md](../raw/2017-06-10-just-mercy.md)).

This pattern recurs: in every act of justice, the person being judged retains their God-given worth. Truth-telling and dignity-protecting are not opposites but partners.
