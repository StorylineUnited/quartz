# Mission

**Summary**: Mission is the outward movement of the Christian life — knowing God and making him known — shaped by a clear biblical vision, pursued through love and [[discipleship]], and expressed through presence as much as proclamation.

## To Know God and Make Him Known

YWAM's 50-year mission statement — "To know God and make Him known" — is treated in the blog as a near-complete summary of Christian mission (source: [2025-05-19-to-know-god-and-make-him-known.md](../raw/2025-05-19-to-know-god-and-make-him-known.md)).

The structure is significant: *knowing* precedes *making known*. Mission flows from encounter. Those who make God known do so not by manufacturing the right message but by overflowing from genuine relationship. The fruit of this double movement is *koinonia* fellowship — others drawn into the same life (1 John 1:3).

## Vision, Mission, Strategy

A complete biblical framework for Christian mission (source: [2020-01-15-vision-mission-and-strategy.md](../raw/2020-01-15-vision-mission-and-strategy.md)):

- **Vision** (the destination): Revelation 21 — new creation, God dwelling with people, all tears wiped away
- **Mission** (the present work): Luke 4 / Isaiah 61 — good news to the poor, release for captives, sight for the blind, freedom for the oppressed
- **Strategy** (the method): Matthew 22 + 28 — love God, love neighbor, make disciples

This provides a canonical check: any church activity can be evaluated against these three biblical anchors. Activity that serves neither the vision, the mission, nor the strategy has drifted.

## Evangelism and Re-evangelism

Evangelism is sharing the Gospel with those who haven't heard it. **Re-evangelism** is sharing the Gospel with those who have — reminding believers of the good news, especially in dark times (source: [2020-10-02-evangelism-and-re-evangelism.md](../raw/2020-10-02-evangelism-and-re-evangelism.md)).

1 Peter 3:15 asks believers to be "ready to give the reason for the hope you have" — and this readiness serves fellow believers as much as strangers. The Gospel is not only the entrance to Christian life but its continuing nourishment.

## Making Disciples as Evangelism

Making disciples is an evangelistic act, not a sanctification program (source: [2018-03-15-making-disciples-is-evangelism-not-sanctification.md](../raw/2018-03-15-making-disciples-is-evangelism-not-sanctification.md)). The four-step sequence:

1. Manifest the Gospel
2. Call people to "Come, follow Jesus" — **this is disciple-making**
3. Connect disciples to communities
4. Organize ministry

Disciples are made quickly — through worship and surrender to Christ's friendship. Formation is longer. Confusing the two either rushes [[spiritual formation]] or mistakes information delivery for mission.

## Making God Known to Two Audiences

Two groups need to know God better (source: [2025-10-21-to-make-him-known.md](../raw/2025-10-21-to-make-him-known.md), citing Acts 17:23):

1. Those whose knowledge of God is legalistic or incomplete
2. Those yearning for goodness, dignity, and justice — seeking without a known object

Paul at the Athenian altar models the second approach: "Let me introduce you to Someone you need to know, and know very well." Making God known means naming what people are already longing for.

## Cultural Kintsugi

Mission in everyday spaces — workplaces, neighborhoods, institutions — is not accomplished with "a bulldozer or a hammer." The *kintsugi* approach: look for cracks (existing brokenness) and fill them with gold (Kingdom values) (source: [2025-06-19-cultural-kintsugi.md](../raw/2025-06-19-cultural-kintsugi.md)).

Small acts — taking responsibility, redirecting toxic dynamics, praying quietly, giving generously — restore integrity and add beauty. "Turning cracks into veins of gold" is the hidden [[sillage]] of Kingdom mission in ordinary life.

## Cultural Fluency as Missional Posture

Being fluent in [[kingdom-of-god]] culture while maintaining literacy of the surrounding world is the missional posture. Ambassadors know both the country they serve and the country they inhabit. This double awareness — knowing where you're from and where you are — enables effective witness without assimilation (source: [2019-08-13-cultural-literacy-and-fluency.md](../raw/2019-08-13-cultural-literacy-and-fluency.md)).
