# Sillage

**Summary**: Sillage — the lingering trail a fragrance leaves — is a recurring metaphor for the relational presence Christians leave behind, and by extension, for the spread of the knowledge of God through witness.

## The Metaphor

*Sillage* (French, pronounced see-YAZH) is a perfume term: the invisible trail of fragrance that follows a person through a room after they have left. It is presence made detectable by absence.

The blog uses this image to ask: what lingering trace do we leave in people's lives? Is our relational presence something others want to invite in again, or do they feel relieved when it ends? (source: [2017-05-05-the-sillage-of-christ.md](../raw/2017-05-05-the-sillage-of-christ.md))

## Sillage as Witness

The sillage metaphor deepens when connected to Paul's language in 2 Corinthians 2:14-16: God "spreads everywhere the fragrance of the knowledge of him." Christians are, in Paul's words, the aroma of Christ — either "the smell of death" to those rejecting God, or "the fragrance of life" to those being saved.

This means Christian witness is not primarily propositional argument but the perceptible presence of someone who knows God. The quality and character of that presence — whether it attracts or repels — depends on how genuinely it reflects the sillage of Christ himself (source: [2025-05-07-sillage.md](../raw/2025-05-07-sillage.md)).

## Sillage and Eternal Life

The blog links sillage directly to [[knowing God]]: "The aroma of the knowledge of God" is the scent of a life that has encountered the living God. Eternal life — knowing God relationally and experientially — leaves a trace. People who live in that knowing spread it, as naturally as a fragrant person spreads fragrance.

[[Mission]] becomes less a program and more a byproduct: those who know God and make him known (source: [2025-05-19-to-know-god-and-make-him-known.md](../raw/2025-05-19-to-know-god-and-make-him-known.md)) do so not by effort alone but by the overflow of encounter. The goal is not to manufacture the right impression but to carry the genuine presence of someone who has been with God.

## Practical Implication

The sillage metaphor redirects questions of Christian witness from tactics to character:

- Not "what should I say?" but "what presence am I?"
- Not "how do I attract people to church?" but "what do I leave behind when I leave?"

It also raises the uncomfortable question: if sillage can be unpleasant — if we can repel rather than attract — then Christian obnoxiousness, judgmentalism, or disengagement has its own fragrance, and it is not the sillage of Christ.
