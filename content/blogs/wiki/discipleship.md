# Discipleship

**Summary**: Discipleship is being trained by Christ through self-denial and obedience — not completing programs — with the goal of becoming fluent in Kingdom culture and ultimately [[knowing God]].

## The Two Decisions

Discipleship distills to two kinds of decision (source: [2017-05-11-the-disciples-two-decisions.md](../raw/2017-05-11-the-disciples-two-decisions.md)):

1. **The first decision**: Follow Jesus (marked by baptism — public commitment and submission)
2. **Every next decision**: Follow Jesus again (marked by ongoing obedience in daily life)

Making disciples means calling people to the first decision and supporting them through every next one. This keeps [[mission]] from being reduced to information transfer; discipleship is an invitation to a living relationship.

## Training, Not Programs

Discipleship is being *trained by Christ*, not enrolled in a curriculum. Luke 9:23 is the operating mandate: "Deny yourself, take up your cross daily, and follow me." The pathway is following Jesus toward Christlikeness — a trajectory, not a checklist. (source: [2021-07-05-discipleship-pathway.md](../raw/2021-07-05-discipleship-pathway.md))

This matters practically: people who complete all the classes without actual formation have not been discipled. Conversely, a person consistently making the next decision to follow — even haltingly — is on the pathway.

## Evangelism vs. Sanctification

Making disciples is distinguished from sanctification (spiritual growth after conversion). "Disciples are made quickly — the fruit of a person's worship and surrender to Christ's friendship." The church's sequence is:

1. Manifest the Gospel
2. Call people to "Come, follow Jesus" — *this* is disciple-making
3. Connect disciples to community
4. Organize ministry

Sanctification (formation, growth in Christlikeness) follows from step 3 onward. Conflating the two causes the church to either rush formation or mistake information delivery for evangelism. (source: [2018-03-15-making-disciples-is-evangelism-not-sanctification.md](../raw/2018-03-15-making-disciples-is-evangelism-not-sanctification.md))

## Cultural Literacy and Fluency

A powerful framework for discipleship uses cultural literacy and fluency as its axis (source: [2019-08-13-cultural-literacy-and-fluency.md](../raw/2019-08-13-cultural-literacy-and-fluency.md)):

- **Cultural literacy**: awareness of a foreign culture — recognizing it, navigating it, but remaining an outsider
- **Cultural fluency**: full immersion — the culture no longer feels foreign; you think and respond from inside it

"The measure of Christian discipleship is Kingdom cultural fluency." A disciple becomes increasingly fluent in [[kingdom-of-god]] culture while maintaining literacy of the surrounding world. This is what it means to be "in the world but not of it" (John 17).

## Identity and Culture: "People Like Us Do Things Like This"

Seth Godin's phrase — "People like us do things like this" — captures the two dimensions of discipleship (source: [2017-09-14-people-like-us-do-things-like-this.md](../raw/2017-09-14-people-like-us-do-things-like-this.md)):

- *People like us* = identity (who I am in Christ)
- *Do things like this* = culture (how Kingdom people live)

Coming to Christ provides a new identity and a new culture simultaneously. Discipleship is learning both: discovering who you are in Christ, and becoming a student of Kingdom culture. The New Testament writers are guides for both.

## Upside-Down Norms

Kingdom discipleship consistently reverses worldly expectations. The Beatitudes (Matthew 5) describe Kingdom favor resting on the humble, the mourning, the gentle, those hungering for justice, the merciful, the peacemakers, the oppressed. "Better said: God's kingdom is the right-side-up one." (source: [2025-11-12-upside-down-kingdom.md](../raw/2025-11-12-upside-down-kingdom.md))

The daily practice is concrete: "Deny myself, take up my cross every day, and follow Jesus' lead, along with others in my church family."
