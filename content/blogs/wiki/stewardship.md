# Stewardship

**Summary**: Stewardship in the blog spans care for creation, care for vulnerable people, and care for the influence entrusted to us — all flowing from the Genesis mandate and the character of God.

## The Genesis Mandate

Stewardship begins with Genesis 1: dominion over creation is not exploitation but responsibility. God entrusts humanity with the care of what he has made. This mandate includes all creation — not only economically productive parts of it — and extends to those who are small, weak, or otherwise unable to advocate for themselves (source: [2017-07-12-survival-of-the-weakest.md](../raw/2017-07-12-survival-of-the-weakest.md)).

A builder who pauses to rescue a worm from desiccating concrete enacts the stewardship instinct: attending to what is vulnerable, without needing it to be "worth it" by any external measure.

## People Stewardship

The language of "Human Resources" treats people as commodities alongside cash and machinery. This is named as a form of dehumanization (source: [2017-07-05-rehumanizing.md](../raw/2017-07-05-rehumanizing.md)):

> "People stewardship must look different than the stewardship of non-human resources."

The alternative: stewarding *influence* — the relational trust and impact we have with people. People are not managed; they are accompanied, seen, and developed. This applies to management, [[community]] leadership, parenting, and friendship.

## Grace as Resource to Steward

In the [[life gauges]] framework, the Responsibility gauge is defined as being "good stewards of God's grace" — receiving what God gives and deploying it well rather than squandering or hoarding it (source: [2019-08-17-summarizing-the-life-gauges.md](../raw/2019-08-17-summarizing-the-life-gauges.md)).

Grace is defined expansively: "unmerited God-given desire, ability, and resources to participate in God's economy as his beloved." Stewardship of grace means using these gifts in service of others and the Kingdom, not keeping them as private possessions.

## Multiplying Effect of Small Acts

Even small acts of care — hand washing, basic hygiene — are forms of stewardship when done with love. "Giving a gift of love to the whole world" through mundane practices illustrates how stewardship multiplies: a single act of self-care becomes a form of care for everyone in contact range (source: [2020-03-07-a-liturgy-of-love.md](../raw/2020-03-07-a-liturgy-of-love.md)).

The [[sillage]] of a stewarding life is invisible but pervasive — a small consistent act of care multiplies through family, friend, and stranger.
